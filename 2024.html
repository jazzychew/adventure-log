When I first did my CAD education as a freshman, I couldn't help but feel like it was quite laborious.

Then, actually building and competing further showed me that the CAD education process was slowing down the team, so I started to conceptualize something new.

By having a real team member make the curriculum for fellow members, I knew it would be more concise and fun to learn. After scripting, filming, and editing all the videos with cute music, the series was complete.

It was nerve-wracking to have people actually use it, but it was easy to see that the change in learning had a positive effect. Freshmen were actually liking to CAD, and they were well-practiced in Onshape and asking questions.

Another part of being CAD lead I loved was getting to participate in the design and build of every part of the robot! It was like a weird robotics dream come true.

One year at home, only two last days of school, and I was already graduating.

I like to think I didn't miss anything in high school. I completely took control of my social life, became a part of a team, started my own club, explored college classes, and more than that, I came out a different person.

Still, I didn't expect to cry so much. Knowing that part of my life where I was surrounded by my kindergarten friends, complained about the schedule the school gave, getting dropped off by my parents was strange.

University was something I was striving for, for years. It seemed to creep into whatever I did. Then, why was I so sad to leave high school? Well, maybe it wasn't sadness. Just, overwhelming fullness.

Knowing I made beautiful friendships and memories was surreal because for nearly a decade prior, I was just the kid with my Papa Smurf stuffy sitting at the elementary school wall, waiting for my dad to pick me up. Now, I was someone who did something. Made friendships, made connections with people who even if we didn't talk again, I would always remember.

Months after my interview at the Minnesota Pollution Control Agency, I was sure they had picked someone else. Getting a call back just after school started had got me jumping with joy.

I've always known I wanted to work with the environment, and the MPCA is one of the largest environmental agencies I could possibly work at. Getting the opportunity to work in the grants services unit was amazing since I could directly see the funding that supports the agency's projects, and I could meet a variety of people in MPCA.

Right when I started, I dove into every project I could. I sharpened my Microsoft skills by creating an Excel sheet full of grant opportunities (which I regularly updated), helping with editing grant forms, and even making a presentation to introduce all of our unit members.

More than the work though, I wouldn't have stayed so long if it weren't for the amazing team I was with. As a newcomer in the professional world, I was nervous, but I always felt supported with the GSU. I couldn't be more grateful for them!
